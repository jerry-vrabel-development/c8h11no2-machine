# LinkedIn Strategy — The C8H11NO2 Machine

> **Status:** Parking doc — for use in a separate conversation
> **Purpose:** Short-form professional content strategy on LinkedIn during the C8H11NO2 Machine build. Complements (does not replace) the dev blog and book.
> **Why separate:** LinkedIn growth is a distinct discipline from book/long-form writing strategy. Different audience, different format, different success metrics. Folding it into the book conversation would dilute both.

---

## The seed idea

Use LinkedIn as the short-form distribution layer for the C8H11NO2 Machine
build. Goals:

- Build professional reputation around "ethical engagement design" as a topic
- Drive readers to the dev blog (where deeper writing lives)
- Build a launch audience for the app (v1.0) and the book (post-v1.0)
- Position the author as a credible voice in product/design ethics

LinkedIn is the right venue because the target audience — product managers,
designers, founders, engineers, design leaders — lives there. It's also where
"professional credibility" gets manufactured in tech: comments on LinkedIn
posts show up in interviews, conference invitations, and book proposal
discussions.

## Context the LinkedIn conversation needs

### The author

- Real name. Public identity. Professional reputation in play.
- Solo developer building C8H11NO2 Machine in evenings.
- Dev background; comfortable writing technical content.
- Existing LinkedIn presence: assume small / unoptimized for planning
  purposes (the strategist should ask if specifics matter).
- Time budget: a few hours/week TOTAL across all writing — book, dev blog,
  AND LinkedIn. LinkedIn must not consume the budget at the expense of the
  other two.

### The project (one-paragraph version)

The C8H11NO2 Machine is a self-aware Progressive Web App that satirizes
variable-ratio reinforcement — users pull a slot/loot mechanism to collect
pixel-art creatures themed to atoms in the dopamine molecule. The satire only
works because the design upholds genuine ethical constraints: no real money,
no FOMO, no dark patterns, transparency mode showing real RNG, plus a
"Compulsion" badge that comes with a gentle note of concern. A book
("Dopamine Development") and dev blog (on GitHub Pages) run alongside the
build, documenting the design philosophy.

### The relationship between LinkedIn, dev blog, and book

These are NOT the same thing and shouldn't be conflated:

- **Book** — long-form, slow, edited, post-v1.0 launch. Written for credibility
  with a broad audience.
- **Dev blog** — medium-form, real-time, written during the build. Captures
  thinking, becomes book raw material, builds an email-list audience.
- **LinkedIn** — short-form, frequent, distribution-focused. Drives traffic
  to the dev blog, builds professional brand, surfaces the project to the
  right people.

LinkedIn posts can pull excerpts from dev blog posts (with a "full version
on the blog" CTA) but shouldn't try to be standalone deep writing. That's
what the blog is for. LinkedIn does the job LinkedIn is good at: short
hooks, professional positioning, network effects.

### Constraints

- Time budget: realistically 1-2 short posts per week MAX, given competing
  demands of app dev, dev blog, and book material capture.
- Cannot soften the ethics framing for engagement on LinkedIn (no "10
  growth hacks" style posts that betray the project's principles).
- Cannot fake authority. Posts should reflect what the author is actually
  doing and learning, not regurgitate generic best practices.
- Real-name identity means everything posted is permanent professional record.
- LinkedIn algorithm rewards behaviors (engagement bait, polls, follow-for-
  follow) that conflict with the project's ethics. Need a strategy that
  works WITH the algorithm without selling out.

### What's already decided / off the table

- The book is happening (separate conversation).
- The dev blog is on GitHub Pages (separate conversation, decided).
- No paid LinkedIn ads / promotion in v0 of this strategy.
- No "personal brand grift" tactics. The brand is the work, not the
  posting style.
- The app must ship first. LinkedIn can't eat that timeline.
